From Rivalry to Unity

History of Joint Planning in the United States Military

How hard-won lessons, determined leaders, and courageous lawmakers transformed a divided force into a unified planning machine

Based on 67 primary sources 1942 to present day
The Problem

War Is Chaos

Millions of people, machines, and supplies must arrive at the exact right place at the exact right time. Without a strict method to organize this chaos, military forces fail. The United States military calls this method the Joint Planning Process: a clear series of steps that helps commanders understand complex problems and design solutions before a battle begins.

The Problem

Four Armies, One Country

For decades, the Army, Navy, Air Force, and Marines operated as entirely separate organizations. They followed different rules, bought different equipment, and built incompatible radio systems. When war came, these divisions turned deadly. The branches each pursued their own missions independently rather than working as one force.

World War Two

Marshall: The Organizer

GM

General George Marshall

Chief of Staff of the Army, 1939 to 1945

People called him "the organizer of victory." Before the war, Marshall completely changed how the Army trained its officers at Fort Benning, Georgia. He hated old-fashioned techniques. He wanted officers who could solve problems quickly on the battlefield and think for themselves, not follow rigid scripts.

When the United States entered World War Two after Pearl Harbor in December 1941, the military had no unified voice. Great Britain already had one: the Chiefs of Staff Committee. Marshall helped create the American version in 1942: the Joint Chiefs of Staff. He served alongside Admiral Ernest King and General Henry Arnold. Together they advised President Franklin Roosevelt on where to send troops and how to divide critical resources like oil and ships. But this early group operated by cooperation, not command. If the branches disagreed, nobody could force a decision.

World War Two

"Not a Voting Organization"

"The Joint Chiefs of Staff is not a voting organization when the interests of the Navy are involved."
Admiral Ernest King, responding to colleagues who wanted to redirect Navy steel toward building landing craft. Three of the four members voted in favor. King refused to accept the result.

This moment captures the core dysfunction. The early Joint Chiefs had no real power to settle disagreements. Each branch protected its own resources, its own plans, and its own pride. If one service refused to cooperate, the group was paralyzed. Admiral William Leahy noted the vote; Admiral King simply rejected it. The Navy's steel stayed with the Navy.

World War Two

Two Wars in the Pacific

Because the military leaders could not agree on a single commander, they literally divided the Pacific Ocean into two separate wars. General MacArthur led one campaign. Admiral Nimitz led another. They planned independently, competed for ships and supplies, and rarely shared intelligence. Meanwhile in Europe, General Dwight Eisenhower managed to run a somewhat unified force, but only through personal diplomacy. The system itself offered no solution.

1947

Unity on Paper Only

What the 1947 Law Created

Congress passed the National Security Act. It created the Department of Defense, officially recognized the Joint Chiefs of Staff in law, and established the Air Force as a brand new separate branch of the military.

What Actually Changed

Almost nothing. The Army, Navy, and Air Force still bought their own weapons, made their own war plans, and competed fiercely for budget dollars. The military was not truly unified. It was merely a loose confederation of separate armies.

The 1947 law was a political compromise. It gave the military a unified structure on paper, but the branches kept all their real power: money, equipment, and the careers of their officers. True unity would take another four decades, two catastrophic disasters, and an act of Congress passed 95 to 0.

The Cold War

Eisenhower's Invisible War

DE

Dwight Eisenhower

Supreme Commander in Europe, then 34th President of the United States

Eisenhower commanded all the allied forces in Europe during World War Two. He experienced the frustration of inter-service rivalry firsthand: getting the Army, Navy, Air Force, and forces from different countries to agree on anything was exhausting. He called this constant internal fighting "the invisible war": the war the public never saw, fought not against the enemy, but against the military's own divisions.

As President, Eisenhower watched the military budget balloon during the Cold War with the Soviet Union. He warned the American people about the growing power of the arms industry, calling it "the military-industrial complex." He knew the separate branches were wasting enormous amounts of money by duplicating each other's work and fighting over resources instead of cooperating.

1958

The 1958 Shake-Up

President Eisenhower told Congress in 1958: "Separate land, sea, and air warfare no longer exists." New weapons like missiles traveled too fast for divided commands. The resulting law made the Secretary of Defense much more powerful and removed the Army, Navy, and Air Force chiefs from the direct chain of command. Orders now flowed from the President to the Secretary of Defense to the field commanders. A massive step forward. But the branches still controlled the money and officer promotions, so loyalty remained with your branch, not the unified force.

1960s - 1970s

Vietnam: Force Without Design

The Vietnam War was the most painful proof that the 1958 reform was incomplete. The United States assembled enormous joint power: air strikes, ground forces, naval patrols, intelligence networks, and support for the South Vietnamese government. But these efforts did not reinforce each other. Bombing decisions were shaped in Washington, far from the battlefield. Each service defended its own traditions. The result was a planning system that could control thousands of missions and still fail to connect military force to a clear political purpose.

The Warning

The Lesson Vietnam Left

The lesson was not simply that the United States lost a difficult war.

The lesson was that the military still lacked the institutions and habits needed to plan jointly: from the highest political objective all the way down to the battlefield. Shifting authority on an organization chart was not enough. The nation needed stronger joint leaders and a more disciplined planning culture.

By the time Vietnam ended, many officers and lawmakers had concluded that the military needed far more than reorganization charts. It needed a fundamentally different culture. But that change would not come from within. It would take two catastrophic failures to force the government's hand.

1980: Iran

Eagle Claw: The Mission

In 1979, militants in Iran took dozens of Americans hostage inside the embassy in Tehran. President Jimmy Carter ordered a rescue mission: Operation Eagle Claw. The plan required the Army, Navy, Air Force, and Marine Corps to fly deep into the Iranian desert, land at a secret spot called Desert One, refuel, then fly to Tehran, rescue the hostages, and escape. It was incredibly complex. And the branches had never practiced it together.

April 24, 1980

Eagle Claw: The Disaster

Evening

Helicopters launch from the carrier

Eight helicopters fly toward Desert One, deep in the Iranian desert.

Mid-flight

A massive sandstorm hits

The pilots cannot see. Visibility drops to near zero. The helicopters fly blind through violent dust and wind.

At Desert One

Helicopters break down

Multiple helicopters suffer mechanical failures. The mission needs a minimum number to proceed. They no longer have enough.

Decision point

The mission is cancelled

Commanders decide to abort and bring everyone home. But the nightmare is just beginning.

During withdrawal

A helicopter crashes into a transport plane

In the darkness and dust, a helicopter collides with an Air Force airplane. Both aircraft are engulfed in flames.

Aftermath

Eight Americans killed

Five Air Force crew members and three Marines die in the fire. The survivors flee into the night. The hostages remain in Tehran.

Admiral James Holloway led the investigation. His team found the root causes: the branches had not trained together, they used completely different procedures, and they had separate commanders with no unified chain of command. The tragedy became a powerful catalyst for change, and directly led to the creation of the United States Special Operations Command.

1983: Grenada

Grenada: Won But Broken

Three years after Iran, the military failed again. President Reagan ordered an invasion of Grenada after a violent coup trapped American students on the island. The United States won the battle, but the planning was chaotic. Army soldiers on the ground could not talk to Navy ships offshore because their radios used different frequencies. In some incredible cases, soldiers had to use local civilian payphones to call their bases back in the United States just to request fire support from ships sitting a few miles away. Four Navy sailors died during the operation.

The Reckoning

Two Disasters, One Truth

Eagle Claw, 1980

Location: Iranian desert
Outcome: 8 Americans killed
Root cause: Branches never trained together
Communication: Separate procedures, separate commanders
Mission: Total failure, hostages not rescued

Urgent Fury, 1983

Location: Grenada
Outcome: 4 Navy sailors killed
Root cause: Branches failed to plan together
Communication: Incompatible radios, soldiers using payphones
Mission: Won despite the chaos, not because of planning

Same failures. Three years apart. The military could not fix itself. The branches protected their own power too fiercely. Congress decided to force the change.

1982 - 1986

Congress Steps In

The disasters in Iran and Grenada enraged Congress. Lawmakers realized the military could not reform itself: the branch leaders protected their own power too fiercely. Congress decided to force the change. What followed was four years of the most bitter political fighting over defense policy since World War Two. Three lawmakers, each shaped by personal experience with war and waste, drove the effort forward against ferocious opposition.

The Reformers

Goldwater: Fury With Purpose

BG

Senator Barry Goldwater

Chairman, Senate Armed Services Committee. Arizona.

Goldwater was famous for his blunt personality and deep conviction that waste and rivalry in the military had to end. He pushed the reform with total determination, refusing to let the opposition slow him down.

The Phone Call

During the writing of the new law, the Navy and Marine Corps fought the reforms with everything they had. The Navy even set up a secret room in the Pentagon specifically to organize resistance against the bill. An Army officer quietly gave Senator Goldwater the telephone number to that room. Goldwater called it himself. When the person answered the phone by saying "Navy War Room," Goldwater took it as confirmation that the reforms were necessary. He pushed the law forward with even greater determination.

The Reformers

Nichols: The Price of War

BN

Representative Bill Nichols

House of Representatives. Alabama. World War Two veteran.

Nichols was a highly decorated gunnery officer who fought in the brutal Battle of Hurtgen Forest in Germany. He knew what bad military planning cost, because he paid the price with his own body.

November 10, 1944

During the fighting in Hurtgen Forest, Bill Nichols stepped on an explosive mine. He was critically wounded. Doctors had to amputate his left leg above the knee. He spent the rest of his life walking on a prosthetic. He spent his entire political career fighting for military veterans and better defense policies. When the reform bill reached the House of Representatives, Nichols sponsored it personally. He understood the deadly cost of bad planning in a way that most lawmakers never could.

The Reformers

Nunn: The Quiet Builder

SN

Senator Sam Nunn

United States Senate. Georgia.

Where Goldwater brought fury and Nichols brought personal sacrifice, Sam Nunn brought quiet logic and relentless research. He was the third pillar of the reform, and in many ways the most essential to its success.

Nunn led major research efforts to prove, with overwhelming evidence, that the military was fundamentally broken. He convinced skeptical lawmakers that the military needed absolute civilian control, not just better cooperation between branches. He built a strong bipartisan partnership with Goldwater to ensure the law could not be defeated by partisan politics. While Goldwater made headlines and Nichols rallied the House, Nunn assembled the votes and crafted the arguments that made the law bulletproof in the Senate.

October 1, 1986

The Vote

383-27
House of Representatives
95-0
Senate

The Goldwater-Nichols Department of Defense Reorganization Act passed with overwhelming, bipartisan support. President Reagan signed it into law. It was the most important military law since World War Two. The four years of bitter fighting were over. The military would never be the same.

Goldwater-Nichols Act

What the Law Changed

One Voice, Not a Committee

The Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff became the single most important military advisor to the President. Before: the Joint Chiefs voted as a group, producing weak compromises. After: the Chairman gave direct, clear advice. One voice instead of a squabbling committee.

Regional Commanders Get Power

The senior generals and admirals in charge of specific regions of the world received total authority over all Army, Navy, Air Force, and Marine forces in their area. The individual branches were stripped of combat power. Their only job now: recruit, train, and equip forces for the regional commanders.

Careers Require Joint Service

An officer could no longer reach the highest ranks by staying in their own branch. They had to serve in a joint assignment and prove they could work with the other services. This broke the culture of branch loyalty that had caused decades of dysfunction.

These three changes attacked the problem at its root. The first fixed decision-making at the top. The second fixed authority in the field. The third fixed the culture by tying personal career advancement to joint cooperation. A law can move authority on paper, but changing promotion rules changes behavior.

The New Era

Crowe: First Under New Rules

WC

Admiral William Crowe

First Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff to serve under the Goldwater-Nichols Act

Crowe mattered not only because of his rank, but because of his temperament. He was known for calm judgment, broad strategic vision, and an unusual ability to make strong personalities listen to one another.

In the old system, a chairman could be trapped between rival service chiefs, unable to override anyone. Under the new system, Crowe could force debates toward one unified military recommendation for the President. He fully embraced his new power and guided the military through a period of massive change. A law can move authority on paper. A respected leader shows an institution how to use that authority in real life. Crowe was that leader.

1989

Panama: A Quick Success

Operation Just Cause

The new law faced its first test in 1989 when the United States launched Operation Just Cause in Panama. A joint task force used the new chain of command to coordinate Army, Navy, Air Force, and Marine forces under a single commander. The operation was brief and successful. It proved the new system could work in combat. But it was a small test. A much larger one was coming.

August 1990

Desert Storm: The Real Test

In August 1990, Iraq invaded the small nation of Kuwait. The United States built a massive global coalition to push the Iraqi army out. General Norman Schwarzkopf was the regional commander. Thanks to the Goldwater-Nichols Act, he had total authority over every Army, Navy, Air Force, and Marine unit in the area. Schwarzkopf was a Vietnam veteran who had seen how divided command distorts war. He believed deeply that one commander should control one campaign. He used his new authority exactly as Congress intended.

Desert Storm

The Black Hole

The Secret Air Campaign Planners

Deep inside a headquarters building in Saudi Arabia, a small group of specialized planners worked in total secrecy. They were known as "the Black Hole." Their job: figure out exactly which targets the airplanes needed to bomb, in what order, and when. They planned the massive air campaign that would destroy Iraq's command systems, radar installations, communications networks, and military infrastructure before the ground war even began.

The air campaign was designed to paralyze the Iraqi military from the top down. By destroying command centers and communications, the Iraqi army in Kuwait would be cut off from its leaders in Baghdad. Soldiers in the field would be unable to coordinate, receive orders, or understand what was happening. Five weeks of precision air strikes would break the enemy's ability to fight before a single ground soldier crossed the border.

Desert Storm

The Jedi Knights

A separate group of Army officers, nicknamed "the Jedi Knights," planned the ground campaign. They designed a brilliant deception: make the Iraqi army believe the main attack would come from the ocean or straight through Kuwait. Instead, they sent massive armored forces hundreds of miles into the open western desert to swing around and attack the enemy from behind. Iraq was looking east and south. The real blow came from the west.

February 1991

100 Hours

<100
hours of ground combat

Operation Desert Storm was a spectacular success. The air campaign lasted five weeks and systematically destroyed Iraq's command and control systems. When the ground war finally began, the western flanking attack shattered the Iraqi army. The ground campaign lasted less than one hundred hours. The Iraqi military surrendered or fled in total defeat. The United States suffered very few casualties. The Goldwater-Nichols Act had been proven in the largest military operation since World War Two.

Lingering Flaws

Still Not Perfect

Paper Delivered by Helicopter

The Air Force controlled the daily bombing target list. But the Navy's computers could not read the Air Force's computers. So the military printed the target list on physical paper and flew it to Navy aircraft carriers in a helicopter. Every single day.

Marines Left Out

The Black Hole and the Jedi Knights did not always share information with each other. The Marine Corps felt completely excluded from the secret planning groups. They had to create their own independent plans, undermining the very unity the new law was built to achieve.

Desert Storm proved the Goldwater-Nichols Act worked: a single commander could win a massive war. But it also proved the military still had a long way to go. The branches still did not fully trust each other. Critical planning groups excluded entire services. And the most basic technology problem, incompatible computer systems, persisted years after Grenada's incompatible radios. The military needed a better, standardized process for making plans.

How Commanders Think

The Bridge

Planning a war is not just checking boxes on a list. It requires deep, creative thinking. The military calls this Operational Art: the bridge between a broad political goal and a specific battle. A political goal might be to stop a country from building illegal weapons. A tactical battle is a fight between two groups of soldiers. Operational Art is the creative approach that connects those two things. It is how a commander uses skill, knowledge, and experience to organize an entire force toward a political objective.

Operational Design

End State: See the Finish

Before a commander writes any plan, they use a framework called Operational Design to understand the environment, define the problem accurately, and visualize the final goal. The military calls this final goal the "end state." If a commander defines the wrong problem, they will write the wrong plan. Once the commander has a clear vision of the end state, the staff begins the formal seven-step Joint Planning Process.

The Joint Planning Process

Seven Steps

The Joint Planning Process is a strict, orderly method. It takes the creative ideas from Operational Design and turns them into a detailed, executable instruction manual. The process forces planners to examine every possible detail and every angle. Because every joint headquarters uses these exact same steps, an Army officer from Texas can travel to a Navy base in Japan and instantly understand how the local planning team works. The common language prevents the terrible communication failures of the past.

Step 1 of 7

Recognize the Problem

The process begins when a high-level authority recognizes a problem. A crisis might happen suddenly (a surprise attack, a massive earthquake) or develop slowly over many years (a rival nation building a new army). The authority looks at the problem, decides the military needs to prepare options, and sends a message to the responsible regional commander. That message initiates the entire planning process.

Step 2 of 7

Understand Everything

This is the most critical step in the entire process. The staff gathers all available information and breaks the problem into categories. Facts are known truths. Assumptions are educated guesses that must be both valid (likely to be true) and necessary (the plan cannot be written without them). If an assumption proves false later, the entire plan might collapse, so the staff constantly checks them and replaces assumptions with facts as soon as possible. At the end, the staff writes a clear mission statement that everyone agrees on.

Step 3 of 7

Invent Multiple Options

The military never creates just one plan. The staff must develop multiple unique options. They look at the troops, ships, and airplanes available and mix them in different ways. Each option represents a fundamentally different approach to solving the problem. This forces the planners to think creatively and gives the commander real choices, not just variations of the same idea.

Step 4 of 7

Test Every Option

This is the most intense and difficult step. The staff tests every option through wargaming: a simulation where one team plays the friendly forces and another team plays the enemy. They test the plan through action, reaction, and counteraction. The process is slow and demands incredible discipline, but it reveals hidden flaws, shows where more supplies are needed, identifies where the enemy is most dangerous, and surfaces the critical decisions the commander will face. Everything is recorded on a timing chart called a synchronization matrix.

Step 5 of 7

Compare and Score

After the wargames, the staff compares the options against criteria chosen by the commander: things like speed, risk to troops, financial cost, or the element of surprise. They build a decision matrix and score each option. This is a highly subjective process. Option A might be very fast but very dangerous. Option C might be cheap but incredibly slow. The staff uses these scores to determine the best path and prepares a formal recommendation for the commander.

Step 6 of 7

The Commander Decides

The staff briefs the commander on all the options, explains the wargame results, presents their decision matrix, and makes a formal recommendation. The commander listens carefully, then makes a final decision. The commander can pick Option A, pick Option B, or combine the best parts of multiple options into an entirely new solution. Once the commander approves a specific concept, it is locked in place. The entire staff shifts to building the final product.

Step 7 of 7

Write the Book

The final step requires massive amounts of detailed work. The staff takes the approved concept and turns it into a gigantic, comprehensive document: the operation plan. The plan includes detailed sections called annexes for every aspect of the operation: intelligence, logistics, medical care, communications, engineering, legal rules, and more. Every single detail is written down. The plan is then published and sent to all subordinate units, who begin their own planning processes to figure out how their specific forces will accomplish their assigned pieces of the larger puzzle.

Desert Storm Walkthrough

Seven Steps in Action

This is how the seven abstract steps played out in a real campaign. The process transformed a general desire to "stop Iraqi aggression" into a precisely coordinated operation that combined diplomacy, air power, land power, naval deception, logistics, and coalition politics into one coherent effort. The elegant concept on a map became thousands of detailed instructions, and the ground war lasted less than 100 hours.

Late 1990s

The Digital Revolution

In the late 1990s, two admirals changed the way the military thought about information. Admiral Arthur Cebrowski believed the old ways of fighting were too brutal and too slow. He looked at the rise of the internet and personal computers in the civilian world and realized something powerful: if a soldier on the ground, a ship at sea, and a satellite in space all shared the exact same picture of the battlefield on a computer screen, they could fight with incredible speed and precision. This concept, called Network Centric Warfare, forced the military to build communication systems that could talk to each other seamlessly, permanently solving the terrible radio and computer problems from Grenada and Desert Storm.

2001 and Beyond

After September 11

Old Wars

Armies on a map. Clear battle lines. Tank divisions against tank divisions. Ships against ships. Campaigns measured in weeks or months. The enemy wears a uniform and holds territory.

New Wars

Scattered networks hiding across mountains, cities, and borders. No battle lines. Small teams linked by shared communications. Campaigns measured in years or decades. The enemy blends into the population.

The terrorist attacks of September 11, 2001 forced joint planning into a new era. In Afghanistan, small teams of special forces, intelligence officers, and local fighters were linked together through shared communications and rapid updates. The opening campaign showed how powerful joint integration could be. But the wars also revealed new weaknesses. In Operation Anaconda, air power was not woven deeply enough into the initial plan. In Iraq, the invasion was brilliantly fast, but planning for what came after was far weaker. The lesson felt familiar: technology can help a commander act faster, but it cannot rescue a plan that was not truly joint from the start.

Hard Lessons

Plans Must Stay Alive

The Old Way

Massive paper documents sitting on dusty shelves. Written once, rarely updated. If a crisis happened, the plan was often completely outdated. Focused only on the military; ignored diplomats, intelligence agencies, and humanitarian groups.

The New Way

Living documents, constantly updated using collaborative technology. If the situation changes, planners rapidly adjust the options. Includes not just the military, but diplomats, allied governments, intelligence agencies, and humanitarian actors. Plans adapt as campaigns change character over years.

Afghanistan and Iraq taught the military that assumptions must be challenged constantly, not just once at the beginning. Modern campaigns may continue for years and change character repeatedly: from high-speed combat to urban security to rebuilding institutions. The military now uses a system called Adaptive Planning and Execution that keeps plans constantly updated. If a political situation shifts in a foreign country, planners rapidly adjust the military options, giving the President flexible, up-to-date choices during a fast-moving crisis.

Today

One World, One Puzzle

Today, the military faces threats that span the entire globe. A conflict in one region can instantly disrupt the economy and security in another. The Chairman of the Joint Chiefs focuses on a concept called Global Integration: looking at the entire world as a single puzzle. A threat in Europe might require troops from America and ships from the Pacific. The military cannot plan for one region in isolation. The Chairman advises the Secretary of Defense on how to move forces seamlessly across the globe to respond to multiple threats at once.

December 2004

Beyond War: The Tsunami

The same seven steps that plan wars also save lives. In December 2004, a massive earthquake in the Indian Ocean created a devastating tsunami that killed hundreds of thousands and left millions without food, water, or shelter. The United States military launched Operation Unified Assistance using the Joint Planning Process. Roads and ports were destroyed, so planners developed options focused on helicopters and ships. Navy helicopters flew directly from aircraft carriers to small, devastated villages, delivering purified water and medicine. The operation proved that the principles of joint planning are universal. They work for dropping bombs, and they work for delivering water.
