# **History of the Joint Planning Process in the United States Military**

## **The Chaos of War and the Need for Order**

War represents the ultimate human chaos. Millions of people must move across the globe. Vast numbers of machines and supplies must follow them. Every person and every machine must arrive at the exact right place at the exact right time. Soldiers must perform dangerous tasks under extreme stress. Without a strict method to organize this chaos, military forces fail. The United States military uses a specific method to organize its forces. This method is the Joint Planning Process.

The Joint Planning Process is a clear series of steps. It helps military commanders understand complex problems. It helps them design creative solutions. It helps them test those solutions before a battle begins.1 This detailed process did not always exist. It was born from bitter experience. It was shaped by devastating failures. It was built by visionary leaders who refused to accept defeat.

For a long time, the different branches of the United States military did not work together. The Army fought on land. The Navy fought at sea. They operated as entirely separate organizations. They followed different rules. They bought different equipment. They built different radio systems. Sometimes, they treated each other as rivals rather than teammates. This fierce rivalry caused massive problems during times of war.

## **World War Two and the First Steps Toward Unity**

The absolute need for joint planning became obvious during World War Two. The United States entered the war after the attack on Pearl Harbor in December 1941\.2 The military faced a massive challenge. It had to fight across the vast Pacific Ocean. It had to fight across the continent of Europe. The United States also had to work closely with the military of Great Britain.

Great Britain already possessed a unified command structure. They called it the Chiefs of Staff Committee. The United States lacked a similar group.2 American leaders quickly realized they needed a unified voice. They needed this voice to talk to the British leaders during major conferences. In 1942, the United States formed a new group. They called it the Joint Chiefs of Staff.2

### **The Influence of George Marshall**

General George Marshall served as the Chief of Staff of the Army. He played a massive role in creating this new unified group.2 General Marshall was a brilliant organizer. People called him the organizer of victory.4 He understood that the military needed flexible leaders. Earlier in his career, he changed the way the Army trained its officers at Fort Benning. He wanted officers to solve problems quickly on the battlefield.5 He hated old fashioned techniques. He wanted officers to think for themselves.

General Marshall served alongside Admiral Ernest King and General Henry Arnold on the early Joint Chiefs of Staff.2 This group advised President Franklin Roosevelt.2 They made heavy decisions about war plans. They decided where to send troops. They decided how to divide critical resources like oil and ships.2

However, this early group had a major flaw. They operated by cooperation. They did not operate by strict command.6 If the Army and the Navy disagreed, the group could not force a decision. Admiral King once proved this point in a dramatic way. The group was discussing the allocation of steel. They needed steel to build ships. Admiral William Leahy noted that three members of the group wanted to use the steel for landing craft. Admiral King refused to listen. He coldly stated that the Joint Chiefs of Staff was not a voting organization when the interests of the Navy were involved.6 The different branches of the military still fiercely protected their own power.

### **Dividing the World**

Because the leaders could not agree, they divided the world. They created separate commands. In Europe, they appointed General Dwight Eisenhower as the supreme commander.6 He commanded a somewhat unified force. However, service politics prevented unification in the Pacific theater. They divided the Pacific theater into two separate commands. General Douglas MacArthur led one command. Admiral Chester Nimitz led the other command.6 This division made planning incredibly difficult.

### **The National Security Act**

After World War Two ended, the United States wanted to fix these glaring problems. Congress passed the National Security Act in 1947\.7 This law created the Department of Defense.8 It also created the Air Force as a brand new separate branch of the military.9 The law officially recognized the Joint Chiefs of Staff in writing.7

The law represented a political compromise. It gave the military a unified structure on paper. However, the Army, the Navy, and the Air Force remained highly independent.6 They still bought their own weapons. They still made their own separate war plans. The military was not truly unified. It was merely a loose confederation of separate armies.6

## **Dwight Eisenhower and the Invisible War**

Dwight Eisenhower was a famous general during World War Two. He commanded all the allied forces in Europe.2 He experienced the frustrations of divided military branches firsthand. He saw how service rivalries wasted time and money. He faced massive challenges getting the different countries and military branches to agree. He once called this internal fighting an invisible war.10

Eisenhower later became the President of the United States. He watched the military budget grow larger and larger during the Cold War against the Soviet Union.11 He warned the American people about the growing power of the arms industry. He called it the military industrial complex.11 He knew the military needed to become much more efficient. He knew the separate branches needed to stop fighting each other.

### **The Reorganization of 1958**

In 1958, President Eisenhower demanded a major change. He sent a powerful message to Congress. He declared that separate land, sea, and air warfare no longer existed.9 He stated that new weapons like missiles traveled too fast for divided commands.13 He demanded that the military form truly unified commands.9

Congress passed the Defense Department Reorganization Act of 1958\.14 This law made the Secretary of Defense much more powerful. It removed the leaders of the Army, Navy, and Air Force from the direct chain of command.6 The chain of command now went from the President directly to the Secretary of Defense, and then directly to the commanders in the field.9

This was a massive step forward. However, the military branches still resisted the changes. The branches still controlled the money. They still controlled the promotions of officers. Because they controlled careers, officers remained intensely loyal to their specific branch rather than the unified military.6 True joint planning was still a distant dream.

## **Vietnam and the Cost of Fragmented Control**

The 1958 law changed the formal chain of command, but the Vietnam War revealed how incomplete the reform really was. During the long struggle in Southeast Asia, the military still found it difficult to give civilian leaders one unified military judgment. The Joint Chiefs of Staff often argued from the perspective of their own services rather than from one integrated concept of war.61 Serious disagreement in war is normal. The real danger was that the system had no reliable way to turn those disagreements into one coherent plan.

The air war over North Vietnam showed this problem in a painful way. Decisions about bombing were often shaped far from the battlefield. Civilian officials in Washington, military leaders in the Pentagon, and commanders in the theater all pushed in different directions.61 The result was a planning system that could be extremely detailed yet still feel disconnected from events on the ground. It was possible to control thousands of sorties and still fail to connect military force to a clear political outcome.

The war in South Vietnam revealed a second problem. The United States could assemble enormous joint power, but it still struggled to integrate that power into one lasting design. Ground fighting, naval patrols, air attacks, intelligence work, and support for the South Vietnamese government did not always reinforce one another smoothly.61 Each service brought valuable capabilities, but each service also defended its own traditions and priorities.

Vietnam therefore became a warning that later reformers never forgot. The lesson was not simply that the United States lost a difficult war. The lesson was that the military still lacked the institutions and habits needed to plan jointly from the highest political objective down to the battlefield.6,61 By the time the war ended, many officers and lawmakers had concluded that shifting authority on an organization chart was not enough. The nation needed stronger joint leaders and a more disciplined planning culture.

## **Tragic Failures Force a Reckoning**

The military slowly improved over the next twenty years. However, the lack of joint planning eventually caused severe disasters. Two major events proved that the military was completely broken. These events forced the government to rewrite the rules of military planning entirely.

### **The Tragedy of Operation Eagle Claw**

In 1979, militants in Iran took dozens of Americans hostage. The militants held the hostages inside the American embassy in Tehran. In 1980, President Jimmy Carter ordered a military rescue mission. The mission was called Operation Eagle Claw.15

The plan was incredibly complex. It required the Army, the Navy, the Air Force, and the Marine Corps to work together perfectly.16 They planned to fly airplanes and helicopters deep into the Iranian desert. They planned to land at a secret spot called Desert One. From there, they would fly to the city, rescue the hostages, and fly out.

The mission ended in a total disaster. The helicopters flew into a massive violent sandstorm.15 The pilots could not see. Several helicopters broke down. The commanders decided to cancel the mission and go home.15 As the forces tried to leave in the dark, a helicopter crashed into an airplane.15 A massive fire erupted. Eight American service members died.15 Three were Marines and five were Air Force personnel.18

The military launched a huge investigation. Admiral James Holloway led the investigation.18 The investigation found terrible problems. The different military branches had not trained together. They had separate commanders. They used completely different procedures.19 The failure proved that the military could not execute joint operations. The tragedy became a powerful catalyst for change.17 It directly led to the creation of the United States Special Operations Command.20

### **The Chaos of Operation Urgent Fury**

Three years later, the military failed again. In 1983, the government of Grenada collapsed in a violent military coup. American medical students were trapped on the island. President Ronald Reagan ordered a military invasion. The invasion was called Operation Urgent Fury.21

The military created a joint task force to run the invasion.21 Admiral Joseph Metcalf led the forces.21 The Marines attacked the northern part of the island. The Army attacked the southern part.21 The Air Force flew airplanes to drop supplies and paratroopers. The Navy provided ships and gunfire support.

The United States won the battle, but the planning was completely chaotic.22 The operation revealed massive communication failures.23 Army soldiers on the ground could not talk to Navy ships off the coast. Their radios used different frequencies. They could not call for help. In some incredible cases, soldiers had to use local civilian public telephones to call their bases back in the United States to request support.

The different branches had failed to plan together.19 Four Navy sailors died during the operation.23 The operation succeeded, but it highlighted the exact same flaws seen in Iran.

## **The Bitter Fight for Reform**

The disasters in Iran and Grenada angered the United States Congress. Lawmakers realized the military could not fix itself. The military leaders protected their own branches too fiercely. Congress decided to force the military to change. This massive effort took four years of bitter political fighting.24 The result was the most important military law since World War Two. It was called the Goldwater Nichols Department of Defense Reorganization Act of 1986\.25

### **The Leaders of Reform**

Three powerful lawmakers led the fight for this new law. Their personal histories shaped their absolute dedication to fixing the military.

Senator Barry Goldwater was a powerful politician from Arizona. He served as the chairman of the Senate Armed Services Committee.25 He was famous for his blunt personality. He was deeply angry about the incredible waste and rivalry in the military.26 During the writing of the new law, the Navy and Marine Corps fiercely opposed the changes. They fought the reforms openly. The Navy even set up a secret room in the Pentagon specifically to fight the new law.24 An Army officer gave Senator Goldwater the secret telephone number to this room. He called the number himself. When the person answered the phone by saying "Navy War Room", Senator Goldwater became predictably enraged.24 He pushed the law forward with total determination.

Representative Bill Nichols was a politician from Alabama. He was a highly decorated veteran of World War Two.27 He fought as a gunnery officer in the brutal Battle of Hurtgen Forest. On November 10, 1944, he stepped on an explosive mine. He was critically wounded. Doctors had to amputate his left leg above the knee.27 He spent his entire political career fighting for military veterans and better defense policies. He knew the deadly cost of bad military planning. He sponsored the law in the House of Representatives.28

Senator Sam Nunn from Georgia was the third crucial leader.29 He provided the quiet, logical arguments for the law.24 He led major research efforts to prove the military was fundamentally broken.24 He convinced other lawmakers that the military needed absolute civilian control.26 He built a strong bipartisan partnership with Senator Goldwater to ensure the law passed.6

### **The Dramatic Changes in the Law**

The Goldwater Nichols Act passed with overwhelming support in 1986\. The vote in the House of Representatives was 383 to 27\.25 The vote in the Senate was an incredible 95 to 0\.25 President Reagan signed it into law. It fundamentally changed the way the military planned and fought wars.25

First, the law made the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff the single most important military advisor to the President.2 Before this law, the Joint Chiefs voted as a group. This often led to weak compromises. After the law, the Chairman gave direct, clear advice.2

Second, the law gave massive power to the combatant commanders.25 These are the senior generals and admirals in charge of specific geographic regions of the world. The law gave them total operational authority over all Army, Navy, Air Force, and Marine forces in their region.25 The individual military branches were stripped of their combat power. Their only job now was to recruit, train, and equip forces for the regional commanders.25

Third, the law changed how officers were promoted.31 It created strict joint officer management policies. An officer could no longer reach the highest ranks by staying in their own branch. They had to serve in a joint assignment.2 They had to prove they could work with the other branches.

Admiral William Crowe was the first Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff to serve under the new rules.32 He fully embraced his new power. He guided the military through a period of massive change.33 He proved that a single, strong military voice was vastly superior to a divided committee.34

Crowe mattered not only because of his rank, but because of his temperament. He was known for calm judgment, broad strategic vision, and an unusual ability to make strong personalities listen to one another.33,34 In the older system, a chairman could be trapped between rival service chiefs. Under the new system, Crowe could force debates toward one military recommendation for the President. That cultural shift was as important as the legal text. A law can move authority on paper. A respected leader shows an institution how to use that authority in real life.

## **The First Major Tests in Combat**

The new laws faced their first tests very quickly. In 1989, the military launched Operation Just Cause in Panama. This operation was a brief success. It utilized a joint task force and proved the new chain of command worked well.19 However, a much larger test arrived shortly after.

In August 1990, the nation of Iraq invaded the small nation of Kuwait.19 The United States built a massive global coalition to push the Iraqi army out of Kuwait. This enormous military campaign was called Operation Desert Storm.19

General Norman Schwarzkopf was the combatant commander for the region. Thanks to the Goldwater Nichols Act, he had total authority over all the forces in the area.19 He created a single, unified plan.

Schwarzkopf also brought hard personal experience to the job. He had served in Vietnam and had seen how complicated command relationships could distort the conduct of war.66 He was demanding, blunt, and sometimes fierce with subordinates, but he believed deeply in the idea that one commander should control one campaign.66 During Desert Storm, he used the authority created by the 1986 reforms exactly as Congress intended. He did not allow the services to fight parallel wars beside one another. He forced them into a common design.

### **The Specialized Planners**

General Schwarzkopf used highly specialized planning groups to build his strategy. One group was known as the Black Hole.19 This group worked in total secrecy deep inside a headquarters building in Saudi Arabia. They planned the massive air campaign. They figured out exactly which targets the airplanes needed to bomb.19

Another group was known as the Jedi Knights.19 These were highly educated Army officers. They had recently graduated from an advanced military school. They planned the ground campaign. They designed a brilliant trick. They made the Iraqi army think the main attack would come from the ocean. Instead, the Jedi Knights sent massive armored forces deep into the western desert to attack the enemy from behind.36

### **A Spectacular Success with Lingering Flaws**

Operation Desert Storm was a spectacular success. The air attacks lasted for five weeks.36 They destroyed the enemy command systems. The ground attack lasted less than one hundred hours.36 The Iraqi army surrendered or fled in total defeat. The United States suffered very few casualties.36

However, the planning was not perfect. The operation proved that true joint planning takes time to learn.19 The different branches still did not trust each other completely. The Black Hole and the Jedi Knights did not always share information. They focused intensely on their own pieces of the puzzle. The Marine Corps felt completely excluded from the secret planning groups. They had to create their own plans independently.19

Furthermore, massive communication problems still existed. The Air Force controlled the master list of daily bombing targets. This list was called the Air Tasking Order. The Navy computers could not read the Air Force computers. The military had to print the daily target list on physical paper and fly it to the Navy aircraft carriers in a helicopter every single day.19

Operation Desert Storm proved that the Goldwater Nichols Act worked. It proved that a single commander could win a massive war. However, it also proved that the military needed a much better, standardized process for making plans.19

## **The Mind of a Commander**

Before exploring the exact steps of the planning process, one must understand how military commanders think. Planning a war is not just checking boxes on a list. It requires deep, creative thinking. The military calls this creative thinking Operational Art.37

Operational Art serves as the bridge between a broad political goal and a specific tactical battle.37 A political goal might be to stop a country from building illegal weapons. A tactical battle is a single fight between two small groups of soldiers. Operational Art is the cognitive approach that connects those two things.37 It is how a commander uses skill, knowledge, and experience to organize forces to achieve the political goal.37

To practice Operational Art, commanders use a concept called Operational Design.1 Operational Design provides a framework for understanding complex problems.39 Before a commander can write a plan, they must truly understand the environment. They must define the exact problem accurately.39 If a commander defines the wrong problem, they will write the wrong plan.

Operational Design helps leaders visualize the final goal. The military calls this final goal the end state.40 Every single action a soldier takes must support the journey toward that specific end state. Once the commander has a clear vision, the staff begins the formal Joint Planning Process.

## **The Seven Steps of the Joint Planning Process**

The Joint Planning Process is a strict, orderly, analytical method.1 It takes the creative ideas from Operational Design and turns them into a detailed, executable instruction manual. The process forces planners to think about every possible detail. It forces them to look at a problem from every angle.

The process consists of seven specific steps.40 Every joint military headquarters in the world uses these same seven steps. This means an Army officer from Texas can travel to a Navy base in Japan and instantly understand how the local planning team is working. The common language prevents the terrible communication failures of the past.

| Step Number | Name of the Step | Primary Purpose | Key Output |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Step 1 | Planning Initiation | Recognize the problem | The order to begin planning |
| Step 2 | Mission Analysis | Understand the facts and limits | The approved mission statement |
| Step 3 | Course of Action Development | Invent multiple different solutions | Several distinct options |
| Step 4 | Course of Action Analysis | Test the solutions in a simulation | Wargame results and matrices |
| Step 5 | Course of Action Comparison | Evaluate the options against each other | A recommendation for the leader |
| Step 6 | Course of Action Approval | The leader makes a final choice | The selected option |
| Step 7 | Plan or Order Development | Write the massive instruction book | The final operation plan |

### **Step One: Planning Initiation**

The process begins when a high level authority recognizes a problem.40 This authority is usually the President of the United States, the Secretary of Defense, or the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff.40

A crisis might happen suddenly, like a massive earthquake or a surprise attack. Or, a problem might develop slowly over many years, like a rival nation building a new army. The high level authority looks at the problem. They decide the military needs to prepare options.40 They send a message to a combatant commander. This message initiates the planning process.

### **Step Two: Mission Analysis**

Mission Analysis is the most critical step in the entire process. The commander and the staff gather all the information they can find.40 They must truly understand what the President wants them to achieve. They must understand the enemy. They must understand the weather and the terrain. The goal is to answer exactly where they are fighting, what they are fighting, and what they must accomplish.41

During this step, the staff breaks the problem down into distinct categories. They look for facts. A fact is a statement that is known to be true.42 For example, a fact might be that the local seaport is fifty feet deep. Another fact might be that a bridge can only hold ten tons of weight.

The staff also creates assumptions.42 An assumption is a logical guess about the future. Because planners cannot know everything, they must make educated guesses to keep the planning process moving forward.42 An assumption must be valid. This means it is likely to be true. An assumption must also be necessary. This means the planners cannot write the plan without it. For example, planners might assume that a neighboring country will allow American airplanes to fly through its airspace. If this assumption proves false later, the entire plan might fail. The staff must constantly check their assumptions throughout the planning cycle.41 They must replace assumptions with facts as soon as possible.42

Next, the staff must identify constraints and restraints.42 These are strict rules forced upon the commander by higher authorities. A constraint is an action the commander must do.43 It dictates an action. For example, the President might order that the military must deliver food to a specific refugee camp. A restraint is an action the commander must not do.42 It prohibits an action. For example, the President might order that the military must not drop bombs near a historical religious building.

Finally, the staff identifies tasks.42 Specified tasks are written directly in the initial orders. Implied tasks are actions the military must do to accomplish the specified tasks, even if no one wrote them down.42 For example, if the specified task is to deliver food, the implied task is to protect the supply trucks from bandits.

At the end of this step, the staff writes a clear, simple mission statement. The commander approves this statement.40 Everyone now knows exactly what they are trying to achieve.

### **Step Three: Course of Action Development**

Once the staff understands the mission, they must invent ways to achieve it. A Course of Action is a potential solution to the problem.40 The military never creates just one single plan. The staff must develop multiple, unique options for the commander.40

The staff looks at the troops, ships, and airplanes they have available. They mix and match these forces in different ways.

For example, imagine the mission is to secure a hostile island.

Option A might be a massive amphibious assault using Marine Corps boats landing on the southern beach.

Option B might be a quiet, stealthy assault using Air Force airplanes dropping Army paratroopers onto the northern mountains.

Option C might involve Navy ships blockading the island while diplomats negotiate.

A good Course of Action must be feasible. This means the military actually has the correct tools to do it.40 It must be acceptable. This means the risk is worth the reward.40 It must also provide the commander with flexibility to handle surprises.40 The staff draws detailed sketches and writes short descriptions of each option.44

### **Step Four: Course of Action Analysis and Wargaming**

This step is the most intense and difficult part of the process. The staff must test every single option they created. They do this through a process called wargaming.45

Wargaming is a simulation.40 It is a conscious attempt to visualize the flow of the battle.40 The staff divides into groups. One group plays the role of the friendly forces. Another group plays the role of the enemy.47

They test the plan using a strict method of action, reaction, and counteraction.40 First, the friendly team describes an action. For example, "We move our aircraft carrier into the narrow strait." Next, the enemy team describes a reaction. For example, "We launch small, fast boats to attack the carrier." Finally, the friendly team describes a counteraction. For example, "We launch helicopters to destroy the small boats before they get close."

This process is slow. It takes incredible discipline. However, it reveals hidden flaws in the plan.47 It shows the planners where they need more supplies. It shows them where the enemy is most dangerous. It helps them identify critical decisions the commander will have to make. The staff records everything that happens in the wargame on a large chart. This chart is called a synchronization matrix.48 The matrix ensures that every action is perfectly timed across the entire battlefield.

### **Step Five: Course of Action Comparison**

After the wargames are finished, the staff possesses massive amounts of data about each option. Now, they must compare the options against each other.40

This is a highly subjective process. The staff uses criteria chosen by the commander.47 The criteria might include things like speed, risk to troops, financial cost, or the element of surprise.44

The staff builds a large decision matrix.44 They score each option against the criteria. Option A might be very fast, but highly dangerous. Option B might be very safe, but incredibly slow. Option C might be cheap, but very difficult to control. The staff uses these scores to determine which option is the absolute best way to achieve the mission. They prepare a formal presentation for the commander. They highlight the advantages and disadvantages of each potential path.49

### **Step Six: Course of Action Approval**

The staff briefs the commander on all the options. They explain the wargame results in detail. They present their decision matrix. They make a formal recommendation.39

The commander listens to the advice. The commander then makes a final decision. The commander can pick Option A. The commander can pick Option B. The commander can also combine the best parts of multiple options to create a brand new solution.

Once the commander approves a specific option, the general concept of the operation is locked in place. The entire staff shifts their focus to building the final product.

### **Step Seven: Plan or Order Development**

The final step requires massive amounts of detailed work. The staff must take the approved concept and turn it into a gigantic, comprehensive document.40 This document is the final operation plan.

The plan includes huge sections of detailed instructions called annexes.50 There is a section for intelligence gathering. There is a section for logistics and supplies. There is a section for medical care and hospital placement. There is a section for communications and radio frequencies. Every single detail is written down clearly.

The staff then publishes the plan and sends it to all the subordinate units.40 The lower level commanders read the overarching plan. They then begin their own smaller planning processes to figure out how their specific units will accomplish their assigned pieces of the larger puzzle.

## **A Real World Walkthrough of the Seven Steps**

The seven steps can sound abstract when they are described in a table. They become much easier to understand when attached to a real campaign. Operation Desert Storm offers one of the clearest examples because the military had to combine diplomacy, air power, land power, naval deception, logistics, and coalition politics into one coherent effort.19,35,36

### **Step One in Practice: Planning Initiation**

The process began when Iraq invaded Kuwait in August 1990\. American leaders immediately faced a strategic question. Should they simply defend Saudi Arabia, or should they eventually eject Iraqi forces from Kuwait by force? The President and senior civilian leaders asked for military options. That request triggered formal planning inside the regional combatant command.19,35

### **Step Two in Practice: Mission Analysis**

Military planners then had to understand the problem in concrete terms. Iraqi forces were dug into Kuwait. Saudi Arabia had to be protected. Coalition unity had to be preserved. Civilian leaders also had political limits. They needed broad international support, and they wanted the campaign to look lawful and disciplined rather than reckless.19,35 These facts shaped everything that followed.

This step also forced planners to identify assumptions and risks. They had to assume the coalition would remain intact long enough to build combat power in the region. They had to estimate how quickly Iraqi defenses could be strengthened. They had to study desert terrain, long supply lines, and the likely reaction of the Iraqi leadership.35 Mission analysis transformed a general desire to stop aggression into a precise military problem.

### **Step Three in Practice: Course of Action Development**

Once the problem was understood, planners developed multiple ways to solve it. A direct frontal ground attack into Kuwait was one possibility. A heavy amphibious threat from the sea was another. A wide flanking movement through the western desert offered a third concept.19,36 Each option promised different advantages. A frontal attack was simple to explain, but it risked heavy casualties against prepared defenses. A wide turning movement offered surprise, but it demanded major logistical preparation.

### **Step Four in Practice: Course of Action Analysis and Wargaming**

This was the point where paper ideas met military reality. Planning teams tested Iraqi reactions to each possible move. If coalition forces attacked frontally, how quickly could Iraqi artillery and armored formations respond? If coalition forces threatened an amphibious landing, how many Iraqi units would stay pinned near the coast? If the main ground attack swung far to the west, could supply convoys keep pace across the desert?19,35,36

The famous deception plan emerged from this kind of testing. Coalition forces convinced Iraq that the decisive attack might come from the sea or from the obvious front in Kuwait. Meanwhile, the true ground offensive was designed to sweep around the Iraqi flank.36 This is what wargaming does at its best. It does not predict the future perfectly. It exposes where surprise, timing, and logistics can change the entire character of a campaign.

### **Step Five in Practice: Course of Action Comparison**

After the analysis, the planners compared their options. The central question was not, "Which idea sounds most dramatic?" The real question was, "Which idea best achieves the political goal at an acceptable cost?" The wide envelopment became attractive because it promised speed, surprise, and a chance to break the Iraqi army without a long, bloody frontal battle.19,36 The maritime threat still mattered, but mainly as a supporting deception rather than as the main blow.

### **Step Six in Practice: Course of Action Approval**

General Schwarzkopf then briefed senior civilian leaders and received approval for the final concept. This did not mean that every detail was frozen forever. It meant the core design had been chosen. The coalition would use a long air campaign to weaken Iraqi command systems and fielded forces. Then it would launch a fast ground offensive built around a sweeping armored attack.19,35

### **Step Seven in Practice: Plan or Order Development**

Only after that decision did the most exhausting work begin. Air tasking orders had to be written. Ground formations had to be moved into position. Fuel, ammunition, medical support, engineers, ports, shipping, and communications all had to be synchronized.19,35 A concept that looked elegant on a map had to be converted into thousands of detailed instructions. This final step is why the Joint Planning Process exists. It turns strategic judgment into coordinated action across an enormous force.

## **Modern Concepts in Military Planning**

The Joint Planning Process constantly evolves. The world changes rapidly, and the military must adapt to new technologies and new threats. In the years following the Goldwater Nichols Act, visionary military thinkers pushed the boundaries of joint planning even further.

### **Network Centric Warfare**

In the late 1990s, two leaders changed the way the military thought about information. Admiral Arthur Cebrowski and Admiral Bill Owens introduced a powerful concept. They called it Network Centric Warfare.51

Admiral Cebrowski believed the old ways of fighting were too brutal and too slow.52 He looked at the rise of the internet and personal computers in the civilian world. He realized that linking military forces together in a massive digital network would create an incredible advantage.53

If a soldier on the ground, a ship at sea, and a satellite in space all shared the exact same picture of the battlefield on a computer screen, they could fight with incredible speed and precision. This concept relied entirely on joint planning and joint technology. It forced the military to build communication systems that could talk to each other seamlessly. It permanently solved the terrible radio problems experienced in Grenada.52

### **The Long Wars After September 11**

The terrorist attacks of September 11, 2001 forced joint planning into a new era. The United States now had to plan not only for short, conventional campaigns, but also for long wars against scattered networks operating across mountains, cities, borders, and villages.62 In Afghanistan, small teams of special operations forces, intelligence personnel, local allied fighters, and aircraft were linked together through shared communications and rapid target updates.63 The opening campaign showed how powerful joint integration could be when information moved quickly.

Yet these wars also revealed new weaknesses. In Afghanistan, Operation Anaconda exposed the danger of incomplete integration. Later studies argued that fixed wing air power was not woven deeply enough into the initial design and had to be used more heavily after the battle began.63 The lesson felt familiar. Advanced technology could help a commander act faster, but it could not rescue a plan that was not truly joint from the start.

The Iraq War produced a different warning. The opening invasion in 2003 displayed extraordinary speed and coordination during major combat.64 Many observers described it as the first fully mature joint campaign built on the post 1986 reforms.65 Army, Marine, Air Force, Navy, and special operations forces all contributed to one fast moving offensive. But overthrowing a government proved easier than stabilizing a country.64 Planning for the period after the initial victory was far weaker than planning for the initial assault.64,65 Headquarters had to adapt from high speed maneuver warfare to a long struggle involving insurgency, urban security, intelligence fusion, and support for fragile local institutions.

These long wars changed planning culture in lasting ways. Planners learned that assumptions had to be challenged constantly, not just once at the beginning of a campaign. They learned that joint planning had to include not only the military services, but also diplomats, intelligence agencies, allied governments, and humanitarian actors.62,64 They also learned that modern campaigns may continue for years and change character repeatedly. A plan therefore had to become a living instrument rather than a static document.

### **Global Integration and Adaptive Planning**

Today, the military faces immense threats that span the entire globe. A conflict in one region can immediately disrupt the economy and security in another region. To deal with this reality, the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff focuses on a concept called Global Integration.55

Global Integration means looking at the entire world as a single puzzle. The military cannot just plan for one specific region in total isolation.55 A threat in Europe might require troops from America and ships from the Pacific. The Chairman advises the Secretary of Defense on how to move forces seamlessly across the globe to respond to multiple threats at once.47

To make this possible, the military uses a system called Adaptive Planning and Execution.67 In the past, military plans were massive paper documents sitting on dusty shelves. If a crisis happened, the plan was often completely outdated. Adaptive Planning and Execution fixes this problem. It uses collaborative technology and standardized procedures to keep plans constantly updated.50,67 If a political situation changes in a foreign country, planners rapidly adjust the military options. This system gives the President flexible, up to date choices during a fast moving crisis.47

## **Beyond Combat: Planning for Disaster Relief**

The Joint Planning Process is famous for winning wars. However, the exact same seven steps are used to save lives during natural disasters. The military calls this type of mission foreign humanitarian assistance.

A profound example occurred in December 2004\. A massive earthquake struck the Indian Ocean. The earthquake created a devastating tsunami.57 Giant waves destroyed coastal communities in Indonesia, Sri Lanka, and Thailand. Hundreds of thousands of people died. Millions were left without food, water, or shelter.

The United States military immediately launched Operation Unified Assistance.57 The military used the Joint Planning Process to respond to the crisis. President George W. Bush directed the military to provide massive compassion and generosity to the victims.58

The planners initiated the process. They analyzed the mission carefully. Their constraints were severe. Roads and ports were completely destroyed by the water. They assumed the local populations needed immediate clean water to prevent deadly diseases. They developed courses of action focusing on helicopters and ships, because land travel was totally impossible.

The military formed Combined Support Force 536\.57 They rapidly deployed aircraft carriers and amphibious assault ships.59 Navy helicopters flew directly from the ships to small, devastated villages.60 They delivered purified water and medical supplies. Because the military had a strict planning process, they brought rapid order to total chaos. They worked smoothly with international aid groups and foreign governments.57 The operation proved that the principles of joint planning are universal. They work for dropping bombs, and they work for delivering water.

## **The Enduring Value of the Process**

The history of the Joint Planning Process is a story of painful growth. The United States military entered World War Two as a deeply divided force. Proud, stubborn service branches refused to share power. Leaders like George Marshall and Dwight Eisenhower fought bitter internal battles to force cooperation. Vietnam showed that partial reform was not enough. Tragic failures in the deserts of Iran and on the beaches of Grenada proved that the old ways were deadly.

The courageous political fights of Barry Goldwater, Bill Nichols, and Sam Nunn broke the power of the individual branches forever. The Goldwater Nichols Act forced the military to fight as a single, unified team. Desert Storm showed the strength of that reform. Afghanistan and Iraq then showed that even strong institutions must keep learning.

Today, the Joint Planning Process stands as a disciplined framework for turning chaos into action. It forces planners to question their assumptions. It forces them to test their ideas in brutal wargames. It gives commanders a common language for thinking through war, crisis response, and humanitarian relief. Whether the mission is breaking an armored army, stabilizing a fractured country, or delivering water after a tsunami, the process exists for the same reason: to connect political purpose to coordinated action before lives are on the line.
